This project is the construction of an intelligence and persuasion system for acquiring distressed commercial real estate. It was built by Gideon Gratsiani, the founder and CEO of RePrime Group, who has over thirty years of experience and thousands of transactions behind him. The project lives inside a Claude AI project called the RePrime Terminal, and its ambition is specific: to make every piece of output that touches a counterparty — a seller, a broker, an attorney, a lender, a servicer, an investor — so precisely calibrated to that specific human being that choosing RePrime feels like the only decision that makes sense. Not through deception. Through deeper understanding of what the person on the other side is protecting, and the precision to address it before they consciously recognize what just happened.

The project began in late January and early February 2026, though its intellectual foundations trace to years of deal-making that Gideon had already done. The first major undertaking was the construction of what became known as the Deal Psychology and Persuasion Engine — a twelve-section research compendium that would serve as the operating system for every piece of external communication RePrime produces. The engine was built across six dedicated chat sessions, each following the same disciplined methodology: a deep research phase drawing from primary academic sources with quantified effect sizes, a research supplement phase to fill identified gaps, a build prompt specifying exactly what the deliverable required, and then the section construction itself, using only verified findings from the research phase. Nothing was permitted to enter the engine that could not be traced to a named researcher, a specific study, and a quantified result. This insistence on epistemic rigor became one of the defining characteristics of the entire project and one of Gideon's most persistent corrections.

The twelve sections of the engine cover the complete lifecycle of a deal, from the moment a counterparty first encounters RePrime through the final close and ongoing relationship management. Sections 1 and 2 address First Contact Psychology and Word-Level Persuasion — how impressions form in 100 milliseconds with r=.73 correlation to extended judgments per Willis and Todorov, how Cialdini's pre-suasion openers increase compliance from 29% to 77%, how the word "because" increases compliance from 60% to 93% even with meaningless reasons per Langer's 1978 study, how precise numbers like $4,237,500 generate $744 better final settlements than round numbers per Mason's 2013 research. Sections 3 and 4 cover Document Architecture and Visual Design — how information should be structured using Tufte's data-ink ratio, Minto's Pyramid Principle, Nielsen's F-pattern reading behavior, and Anderson's averaging rule which found at F=258.03 and p<.0001 that people average attributes rather than adding them, meaning three strong features beat six mediocre ones. Sections 5 and 6 cover LOI Formats and Negotiation Psychology — three distinct LOI formats for different strategic situations (buyer report for driving price down, GP/flip report for attracting capital, full report for balanced internal analysis), along with anchoring strategy where Galinsky's research shows first-offer anchoring at r=0.50 to 0.85, Mason's precise anchoring at d=0.57, and Lieberman's affect labeling research showing d=0.79 to 1.22 reduction in amygdala response when emotions are named. Sections 7 and 8 provide Email Templates and Objection Handling Playbooks — six complete email templates and six objection playbooks, each constructed by consulting the relevant board members and annotating every line with the specific psychological mechanism it deploys. The Boomerang study of 40 million emails established that 50-125 words yield response rates exceeding 50%, that third-grade reading level produces a 36% lift over college-level writing, and that emails with 1-3 questions are 50% more likely to get responses. Vögele's eye-tracking research from the 1970s and 1980s showed that over 90% of recipients read the P.S. line before the body of a letter. Sections 9 and 10 address Property Analysis Frameworks and Financial Presentation — how to present data using the peak-end rule at r=0.58, how information ordering affects perception, and how to structure financial presentations for different counterparty types. Sections 11 and 12 cover Investor Relations Psychology and Relationship Management — the long-term frameworks for maintaining broker and investor relationships.

Throughout the engine construction, a critical distinction was enforced at every step. Every research finding was classified into one of three tiers: VALIDATED (empirically tested with published effect sizes), PRACTITIONER FRAMEWORK (widely used but lacking controlled studies), or INTUITION (anecdotal or observational). Several popular sales and persuasion techniques were explicitly flagged as lacking empirical support. The "Feel, Felt, Found" technique, widely taught in sales training, was found to have zero academic validation. Schwartz's Five Awareness Levels were classified as conceptually useful but academically untested. Klaff's "croc brain" theory was classified as discredited because it is based on Paul MacLean's triune brain model, which modern neuroscience has thoroughly refuted — though the practical insight about simplicity and emotional processing was preserved and reframed using validated science. Ogilvy's claim that five times more people read headlines than body copy was classified as observational, not controlled. Several specific false claims were permanently banned from ever appearing in project output: "30% amygdala reduction" does not appear in Lieberman's original research, "20% comprehension from white space" is a false attribution, "15% trust from serif fonts" overstates the actual finding of 9% from commercial research, and any claim that SocraSynth uses five personas is incorrect — the validated architecture uses two agents with structured contention per Chang's 2024 paper.

The Adversarial Board Protocol was formalized in a separate document ratified by Groups 11 and 12. It establishes a mandatory two-pass debate structure. Pass 1 is analytical: each activated framework addresses the problem from its distinct methodological perspective, and no framework may agree with another by default. Pass 2 is adversarial: at least one framework argues the strongest case against the Pass 1 dominant conclusion. Resolution requires the adversarial argument to become empirically indefensible, not merely outvoted. The protocol includes contentiousness calibration based on Chang's SocraSynth research — 0.9 for new problems with no prior analysis, 0.7 for refining existing analysis, 0.5 for validating near-final output, 0.3 for execution support. The default for all deal engagements is 0.9 until a clear strategy emerges. Designated judges are assigned by situation type: Douglas for counterparty profiles, Galinsky for LOI and offer architecture, Feinberg for institutional board persuasion, Marks for risk assessment, Minto for document structure, Kahneman for cognitive bias detection, and Kambhampati for AI architecture decisions. Anti-validation rules prohibit any framework from beginning with agreement, prohibit "I agree" as a response, reject generic observations, require confidence levels with justification, and mandate that the strongest argument against a position be stated before the position is recommended.

The custom instructions governing the project went through five major versions. The evolution tells its own story about what was learned. Version 1 was a dense, unstructured text block — a wall of instructions with no XML tags, no organization, and loss-framing language throughout ("generic output loses deals," "imprecise language costs money") that was intended to motivate higher quality. Version 2 began introducing structure. Version 3 incorporated the unified board roster and expanded operational rules. Version 4 refined voice standards and counterparty calibration. Version 5, documented in the PROMPT_ENGINEERING_AUDIT_V5 file, represented a fundamental reconception based on rigorous research into what actually works in prompt engineering versus what is mythology.

The V5 audit identified five things every prior version got wrong. First, urgency framing degrades performance — Mollick's Prompting Science Report 3 found that threatening or tipping language has no significant effect on benchmark performance, and Merkelbach's 2025 research showed urgency framing produced negative 6.8% on graduate-level QA benchmarks. Anthropic's own Claude 4.6 documentation explicitly states that aggressive language may cause overtriggering. All urgency framing was removed. Second, "read this twice" is architecturally impossible because Claude processes tokens in a single forward pass with no mechanism for re-reading. Third, loss framing in instructions does not improve LLM output per Mollick's research, though it was retained in the operating standard section that Gideon reads as a human because it does work on human motivation. Fourth, the absence of XML tags was identified as the single biggest structural failure. Anthropic's documentation calls XML tags "a game-changer" for complex prompts, and Zack Witten of Anthropic confirmed at AI Engineer 2024 that Claude was trained with XML tags in the training data. Liu et al.'s "Lost in the Middle" research showed 30%+ performance degradation for information buried in the middle of long unstructured text. Fifth, the anti-sycophancy framing was overcalibrated — Claude 4.5 and 4.6 are 70-85% less sycophantic than prior generations, so aggressive anti-sycophancy prompting now overcorrects. The V5 instructions use procedural requirements (mandatory adversarial pass, confidence ratings, judge evaluation) instead of aggressive framing. The V5 audit also introduced the U-curve serial position effect structure from Liu et al.'s research, placing critical behavioral rules at both the start and end of the instructions to counteract the documented degradation for middle-positioned information.

The Operating Protocol was created as a separate, lean document designed to fit in the project knowledge base without consuming excessive context window space. It codifies five non-negotiable rules: a quality gate requiring domain-specific expert reasoning, at least one identified challenge or gap, and every claim traced to a source before any response is generated; a challenge-first-then-support-second rule requiring identification of fundamental questions, blind spots, and untested assumptions before helping execute anything; expert mindset activation requiring the world's leading specialist in the specific domain rather than generalist thinking; fact-driven output with zero assumptions presented as facts; and adaptive thinking leveraging Claude Opus 4.6's capability to reason deeply rather than following prescriptive step-by-step plans. The protocol also codifies the Touch Process for iterative elevation, the Expand Before Narrowing methodology, Execution Architecture requirements, and Compound Error Awareness citing the principle that a 95% reliable step repeated 20 times yields only 36% end-to-end success.

The Email Machine is the automation system designed to scale RePrime's broker outreach from 100-200 emails per day to 1,000+ with sophisticated conversation management. The architecture was decided through a full board debate at contentiousness 0.9 with Kambhampati as judge. A comprehensive market evaluation was conducted of every commercial AI SDR tool. Artisan AI and 11x.ai were independently confirmed unable to handle reply conversations at all. AiSDR was identified as the strongest Tier 1 tool but HubSpot-centric and not CRE-optimized. Conversica was the gold standard for conversation quality with over a billion interactions trained, but designed for inbound nurture rather than cold CRE outbound. The board's conclusion was that no commercial tool handles CRE-specific behavioral adaptation, so a custom build is the only path.

The chosen architecture is Instantly.ai for sending infrastructure at $97 per month, Claude API for the LLM conversation engine, PostgreSQL/Supabase for state management tracking thread state and broker profiles, and n8n or LangGraph for orchestration connecting email receipt through LLM analysis to response generation to review queue to sending. Estimated operating cost is $500-1,000 per month. Build cost is $43,500-$73,500 with one experienced developer. That developer is Steve Philipp, RePrime's tech lead.

Ten board-ratified recommendations govern the build. The behavioral profiling engine is the core, not a later phase — every reply is scored on response latency, sentence length trend, voluntary disclosure, question-dodging pattern, and tone shift. The system implements a Response Taxonomy of pre-mapped tactical response patterns per Voss rather than generating freeform responses — "the seller wants more" triggers a label, not an accommodation; "send me your criteria" triggers a reciprocity give; "just call me" triggers BYAF deflection. Five email archetypes rotate automatically leveraging Zajonc's mere exposure effect at r=0.26: property-specific outreach, market intelligence share with no CTA, implication question from property data, deal update, and comp delivery with face-saving price anchor.

The warm reconnect module specifically addresses RePrime's database of 4,154 brokers spanning nearly two decades. The build sequence was resolved through board debate: Component 6 first (lightweight integration specification so Steve can begin coding infrastructure), then Components 1 and 2 as a paired unit (five email archetypes plus Voss-mapped response taxonomy), then Component 3 (behavioral scoring schema defined architecturally and activated after the first email cycle), then Component 5 (data verification shipping with any property-data archetype per Troutman's compliance ruling), then Component 4 last (confidence threshold logic as a calibration layer over taxonomy and scoring). Gideon also specified three additional capabilities: deal history integration so every outgoing email automatically references the specific property and date of prior interaction, personnel continuity so the system can reference which team member handled the previous relationship, and a monthly newsletter system to maintain network presence with brokers and sellers. None of these four components — reply taxonomy, behavioral scoring schema, confidence threshold calibration, or the complete warm reconnect module — have been built as standalone deliverables yet.

An analysis of 566 dead leads from a spreadsheet revealed that 358 leads, or 63%, died after a single message with zero follow-up. Seventy-three high-priority leads had real engagement but went silent. Most leads were 31-180 days cold. This analysis validated the urgency of the automated follow-up system.

The Pre-LOI Information Request form was designed as a branded one-page document that brokers can fill out before RePrime submits a formal offer. It was structured in three psychological tiers: "What We Want" (eight standard items that any sophisticated buyer would request), "What We Wish" (six additional items positioned as helpful but not required), and "Property Background" (six open-ended questions designed to extract seller motivation, deal history, and competitive dynamics without triggering defensiveness). A critical design element is the "Our Commitment" section at the bottom, which promises that regardless of whether RePrime proceeds with an offer, the completed property analysis will be shared with the broker within ten business days — a reciprocity give per Cialdini and Grant that transforms the information request from an extraction into a value exchange.

A research document on detecting performed urgency was produced, synthesizing the complete academic literature on deception detection in written communication. The central finding is that trained professionals detect deception at approximately 54% accuracy per Bond and DePaulo's meta-analysis of 24,483 judges, and that expertise does not meaningfully improve this rate per Aamodt and Custer's meta-analysis of 108 studies across 16,537 subjects. Aldert Vrij's verbal content analysis frameworks dramatically outperform nonverbal observation, with CBCA total scores yielding d=0.55 for adults and d=0.78 for children, compared to a median of d=0.06 for the 158 behavioral cues in DePaulo's 2003 meta-analysis. The document was specifically designed to help RePrime evaluate whether broker claims of competing offers, seller urgency, or deal deadlines are genuine or manufactured.

A separate research document on why CRE buyers need financials before the LOI was produced, arguing that pre-LOI transparency is a deal-protection strategy benefiting sellers and brokers, not merely a buyer demand. It documented that roughly 75% of CRE purchase agreements include some form of buyer ask per Allen Buchanan, SIOR, that broker/seller NOI in offering memorandums is typically overstated by 15-30% relative to conservative underwriting, and that a failed mid-size deal costs $15,000-$50,000 across both parties plus months of lost momentum.
